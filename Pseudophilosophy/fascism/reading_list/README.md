If you read just ONE book on fascism, it should be Robert Paxton’s The Anatomy of Fascism. 



Paxton is absolutely the preeminent historian of fascism, and he walks through the history that links every fascist movement really clearly. Paxton’s personal story is actually really interesting too: as we say in the video, most serious historians of fascism are reluctant to apply the label to today, and Paxton used to be in that camp as well, until he very publicly changed his mind after the January 6 riots in 2021. That isn’t covered in the book, which is from 2005, but it’s a useful piece of context for understanding his work.




NEXT STOPS
🥈+ 🥉

Your next stop should be Roger Griffin’s The Nature of Fascism. 



Griffin, who’s in our video, is the author of the most widely accepted definition of fascism — a revolutionary ultranationalism that promises rebirth — and this is the book that contains it. We also recommend just searching Roger Griffin on YouTube and watching any of his very insightful interviews and appearances — unlike Paxton, he’s still young enough to be an active participant in the ongoing fascism debates (he spoke to us, after all), and his insights are a real gift.

➡️

The essay collection Did it Happen Here, edited by Daniel Steinmetz-Jenkins, is next. 



This one is packed full of fascism experts, and it goes head-on at the main question on everyone’s minds: not just, what is fascism, but, is THIS fascism? Are we experiencing it? This is the best book to read to get a sense of the real range of scholarly opinions; from someone like Richard Evans, maybe the preeminent living scholar of Nazism, who argues vehemently that we AREN’T seeing fascism; to someone like Jason Stanley, to whom it’s screamingly obvious that yes, it’s fascism.



HONORABLE MENTIONS
🏅

If you’re STILL looking for more, these three should round out your set. 

First, Ruth Ben-Ghiat’s Strongmen: Mussolini to the Present. 



Ben-Ghiat herself is circumspect about using the word fascism, but she’s one of the top living experts on Mussolini, and her book traces his rise and those of his imitators. 

➡️

Second is our friend Federico Finchelstein’s The Wannabe Fascists, which we mention in the video, and which is his stab at defining what’s actually happening today. 



➡️

And finally, Richard J. Evans’s The Coming of the Third Reich: it’s just the first in his trilogy about Nazi Germany, but it’s the one to read if you want to understand how fascists come to power. We relied really, really heavily on it when writing this video.



➡️

There are also really popular books like Tim Snyder’s On Tyranny and Jason Stanley’s How Fascism Works, which have really exploded in the mainstream and have shaped a lot of the media conversation around fascism today. They were some of our starting points when we began researching, but as we say in the video, we did come to learn that their points of view are a little more on the fringes of the scholarly consensus. Still, they’re quick reads, and useful for understanding how a lot of the liberal establishment is approaching the fascism debate.
